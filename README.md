# Circulating-protein-on-multistage-liver-disease

# ABSTRACT
Background & Aims: Reliable biomarkers that track liver disease progression from steatosis to cirrhosis and hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) are lacking. We aimed to identify circulating proteins with protective effects across the inflammation-to-cancer continuum and evaluate their clinical utility.
Methods: We performed proteome-wide Mendelian randomization by integrating eight plasma proteomic datasets with 13 liver disease genome-wide association datasets using Bonferroni correction. Neurocan (NCAN) was prioritized and evaluated in a NASH‑HCC mouse model to characterize its expression during disease progression. Associations with incident liver disease, liver-related mortality, and disease diagnosis were examined in UK Biobank participants (n=51,966) and a Chinese cross-sectional cohort (n=202) using Cox and logistic regression. The incremental value was assessed using the area under the receiver operating characteristic curve (AUC).
Results: Twenty causal protein–disease associations were identified. NCAN was consistently protective across steatosis, cirrhosis, and HCC (Padj<.05). In mice, circulating NCAN levels were inversely correlated with steatosis, inflammation, and fibrosis (P<.05). In humans, higher NCAN levels were associated with lower incidence of multiple liver diseases and reduced liver‑related mortality. Integrated NCAN with the LiverRisk score improved discrimination for incident liver disease (time‑dependent AUC 0.67-0.76 over 1-16 years) and liver‑related mortality (AUC 0.75-0.80 over 3-16 years) in the UK Biobank. In the Chinese cohort, NCAN alone demonstrated a strong diagnostic performance for significant fibrosis (AUC=0.91) and HCC (AUC=0.90). Adding NCAN to the LiverRisk score further improved fibrosis diagnosis (AUC=0.96), and integration with aMAP enhanced HCC diagnosis (AUC=0.89).
Conclusions: Circulating NCAN is a protective inflammation‑to‑cancer biomarker for liver disease, providing prognostic, diagnostic, and risk‑stratification value across the entire disease spectrum.
Keywords: Liver Disease; Proteomics; Inflammation-to-Cancer Transition; Risk Prediction; Biomarkers.
